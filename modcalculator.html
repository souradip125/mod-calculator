Do You Know About Modulo operation?

In computing, the modulo operation returns the remainder or signed remainder of a division, after one number is divided by another (called the modulus of the operation). Given two positive numbers a and n, a modulo n (abbreviated as a mod n) is the remainder of the Euclidean division of a by n, where a is the dividend and n is the divisor.[1] The modulo operation is to be distinguished from the symbol mod, which refers to the modulus[2] (or divisor) one is operating from. For example, the expression "5 mod 2" would evaluate to 1, because 5 divided by 2 has a quotient of 2 and a remainder of 1, while "9 mod 3" would evaluate to 0, because the division of 9 by 3 has a quotient of 3 and a remainder of 0; there is nothing to subtract from 9 after multiplying 3 times 3. (Here, notice that doing division with a calculator will not show the result of the modulo operation, and that the quotient will be expressed as a decimal fraction if a non-zero remainder is involved.) Although typically performed with a and n both being integers, many computing systems now allow other types of numeric operands.
